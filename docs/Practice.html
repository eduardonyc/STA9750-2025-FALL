PART 2: Updated Specific Questions

Overarching Question:

“Did COVID-19 reshape the drivers of housing affordability differently across NYC neighborhoods, revealing distinct local market dynamics?”

Refined Specific Questions (Final Version)

1. Transportation Accessibility (Tiffany)

“Did the relationship between transit accessibility and property values weaken post-COVID (2020-2025) compared to pre-COVID (2015-2019) across NYC’s Community Districts, and did this weakening vary spatially?”

Operationalization: - Unit of analysis: 59 Community Districts - Transit accessibility: Average distance to nearest 3 subway stations per CD - Property values: Median sale price per CD from DOF Rolling Sales - Time periods: Pre (2015-2019) vs. Post (2021-2025) - Test: Price ~ TransitDistance × PostCOVID × Borough + Controls

Expected finding: Transit-price correlation weakened more in Manhattan/Downtown Brooklyn than outer boroughs

2. Crime and Safety (Kelly)

“How did crime’s impact on housing costs change from pre-COVID (2015-2019) to post-COVID (2021-2025) across NYC’s Community Districts, and did this shift vary by initial crime levels?”

Operationalization: - Unit of analysis: 59 Community Districts - Crime measure: Major felony rate per 1,000 residents per CD (from NYPD data) - Property values: Median sale price per CD - Test: Price ~ CrimeRate × PostCOVID × InitialCrimeLevel + Controls

Expected finding: Crime became a stronger predictor of prices post-COVID, especially in previously low-crime areas

3. Educational Attainment (Eduardo)

“Did the strength of the relationship between neighborhood educational attainment and median home prices change post-COVID, and did this change differ across NYC’s Community Districts?”

Operationalization: - Unit of analysis: 59 Community Districts
- Education measure: % Bachelor’s degree or higher from ACS - Pre-COVID: 2015-2019 ACS - Post-COVID: 2019-2023 ACS (or 2018-2022 ACS) - Property values: Median sale price per CD - Test: Price ~ EducationLevel × PostCOVID × Borough + Controls

Expected finding: Education premium weakened in Manhattan but strengthened or stayed stable in outer boroughs

4. Commercial Corridor Vitality (Madison)

“How did the relationship between commercial corridor vitality and nearby residential property values shift post-COVID across NYC’s Community Districts?”

Operationalization: - Unit of analysis: 59 Community Districts - Commercial vitality: - Active businesses per square mile (from Business Licenses) - OR commercial property density from PLUTO - Property values: Median residential sale price per CD - Test: ResidentialPrice ~ CommercialDensity × PostCOVID × Borough + Controls

Expected finding: Commercial vitality became less important for residential values in Manhattan, more important in outer boroughs

5. Residential Density (Jonathan)

“Did high residential density become a penalty for property values post-COVID across NYC’s Community Districts, and did this ‘density penalty’ vary by baseline density level?”

Operationalization: - Unit of analysis: 59 Community Districts - Density measure: Residential units per acre from PLUTO - Property values: Median sale price per CD - Test: Price ~ Density × PostCOVID × InitialDensityLevel + Controls

Expected finding: Density penalty emerged in highest-density CDs (Manhattan core) but not in moderate-density areas

How These SQs Answer the OQ

The OQ asks: Did COVID reshape drivers differently across neighborhoods, revealing distinct markets?

Each SQ tests one driver:

SQ Driver Tested Spatial Variation Expected? Market Fragmentation Evidence
1 Transit access YES (weakened in core, stable elsewhere) CBD-periphery decoupling
2 Public safety YES (more important post-COVID, varies by baseline) Safety-seeking segmentation
3 Education quality YES (matters differently by borough) Family-priority segmentation
4 Commercial amenities YES (less important in Manhattan) Amenity preference shifts
5 Density YES (penalty in core, neutral elsewhere) Density preference restructuring

Integration logic: - If 0-1 relationships changed differently → Weak fragmentation - If 2-3 relationships changed differently → Moderate fragmentation (your likely finding) - If 4-5 relationships changed differently → Strong fragmentation - Pattern of which changed where reveals nature of new market structure

PART 3: Geographic Scale Decision Slide

Here’s content for your presentation slide:

SLIDE CONTENT: “Geographic Scale: From Boroughs to Neighborhoods”

Title Slide:

Refining Our Analytical Approach:
Why We Analyze Community Districts, Not Boroughs

Visual Layout:

[Left Side - Problem]

Initial Proposal: Borough-Level Analysis - 5 Boroughs = 5 Observations ❌ - Insufficient statistical power - Cannot detect meaningful patterns - Masks within-borough variation

[Visual: Simple map showing 5 boroughs in solid colors]

[Right Side - Solution]

Refined Approach: Community District Analysis - 59 Community Districts = 59 Observations ✅ - 10x increase in statistical power - Captures neighborhood-level variation - Enables robust spatial analysis

[Visual: Map showing 59 CDs in varied colors]

Middle Section - Justification:

Why Community Districts?

Criterion Boroughs ❌ Community Districts ✅
Sample Size n = 5 n = 59
Statistical Power Inadequate for regression Sufficient for interaction models
Spatial Granularity Too coarse Captures neighborhood variation
Policy Relevance Limited actionability City planning uses CDs
Data Availability All sources available Most sources aggregate to CD

Bottom Section - Methods Note:

Analytical Implications:

✅ Enables difference-in-differences approach (instructor’s suggestion) - Compare similar CDs that experienced different changes

✅ Tests spatial heterogeneity rigorously - Can statistically test if relationships vary across space

✅ Sub-borough granularity without excessive complexity - More than census tracts (2,100) but meaningful units

✅ Aligns with instructor feedback > “Try to go sub-borough. If you work on a borough-level basis, you really only have 5 observations, which isn’t much…”

Alternative Slide Version (More Visual):

[Top Half]

The Problem with Borough-Level Analysis

Borough Analysis: n = 5

Regression with 5 observations: - Cannot control for confounders - Cannot detect interaction effects
- Results not statistically reliable - Masks neighborhood differences

Example: Manhattan - Upper East Side ≠ Washington Heights - But borough-level treats them the same


**[Bottom Half]**

**Our Solution: Community District Analysis**

[Graphic showing]:

Community District Analysis: n = 59

Enables robust statistical analysis: ✓ Proper regression with controls ✓ Interaction models (Factor × COVID × Space) ✓ Captures within-borough variation ✓ Sufficient power to detect effects

Example: Manhattan’s 12 CDs - Upper East Side (CD 8): High income, transit-rich - Washington Heights (CD 12): Working class, transit-dependent - Can now compare their different COVID responses
